As the final, bittersweet month of college approaches, I feel called to reflect on what this education meant to me. Some questions that come to mind are:
- Who am I now, with the knowledge and skills acquired?
- Am I prepared to be a good and effective citizen, worker, friend?
It is then I realize that if the answers to these questions are unsatisfactory ("not a person I'm reasonably happy/proud with given the circumstances" or "no", respectively), then perhaps education has failed. Thankfully, my answers are not totally unsatisfactory--in fact, they are pretty satisfactory. A success in my eyes.

Now, a big part of why was my time in college. It was transformative for me. As college does, I was granted a great level of autonomy suddenly all at once, faced extraordinary challenges (comparatively from before)--both mental and physical, and found loving communities where I was incubated, matured and came out having a lot of fun. Within all of this, I pursued a combined major in Computer Science and Philosophy. 

Pursuing this major alone was a big part of what made education work for me. Computer Science is my "practical passion"--something that can make me a valuable producer in society (though, maybe not anymore after AI is done self-mutilating it's mother-industry) and help financially sustain my needs. As for the Philosophy side of my major, it is not immediately as clear in the practical sense that it makes me a valuable producer. However, it is the thing that has become foundational to nearly every other aspect to being a good and effective citizen, friend and person. Without it, I would feel a lot less confident to being those things, feel significantly less satisfactory to my education.

I feel so strongly about this that I would like to put forth this claim: Philosophy should be a mandatory subject for all education.

For the sake of brevity for the claim, I didn't specify what part of philosophy. Though, it makes a big difference what kind of philosophy I mean. I will carve out the following:
- Logic: How to determine and create valid inference
- Moral Theory: Metaphysics of morality (what is morality and does it exist?), normative ethics (what is good and bad?), some practical ethics of personal choice (e.g. medical ethics, business ethics, environmental ethics, tech ethics, social ethics and political ethics)
- Some selection of:
	- Political philosophy
	- Philosophy of economics
	- Indigenous or eastern philosophy
	- Epistemology (philosophy of knowledge)
So, not:
- History of philosophical thought (e.g. doing big interpretative work into Plato, Kant, Nietzsche, etc.)
as I think while it is helpful to go through the great thinker's lines of reasoning--and certainly their works will appear in the other forms of philosophy I mandate--it is not necessary as part of what I'll argue is that philosophy develops foundational critical thinking skills and rationality, not that philosophy is a ball of knowledge to obtain.

To support my claim, I will do the following work for each of the subjects. I will give a little tour of them, and then a formal definition. Then I'll provide some empirical and anecdotal evidence to demonstrating why they are necessary for the education of all people. For something to be necessary for the education of all people, it should sufficiently allow for an individual to answer the two questions posed in the beginning of this paper in a satisfactory way. By the end of this, you will hopefully become inspired to learn a little bit of them yourself, or convinced that future children should have a form of education that has these elements. As you can see, there are is no "hard" evidence or lines of reasoning as might be required if I were to actually submit this to some school board. Nevertheless, the purpose of this is more so to plant a reasonable idea in people's heads.

First, let's start with logic.

We use and hear the term "logic", or "being logical" everywhere, particularly in places where people are trying to prove a claim like in politics. People use this term liberally in arguments: "You're not being logical!" This is technically an very grave claim--more grave that people think--to make against your opponent in an argument. It means your opponent's argument could never be "valid", not even if all your claims are true. You might ask, how can that be? But let's see this example of an argument: All humans are mammals and Socrates is a man. Therefore, Paris is the capital of France. This argument is not logical because the conclusion (Paris is the capital of France) does not follow from the premises (1. All humans are mammals, 2. Socrates is a man). So even given all of these statements happen to be true, the argument is not valid and so you're not bound by logic to believe the conclusion from this argument alone (even in this instance in which we know the conclusion is true.) That's powerful. So if someone is trying to convey some truth that should be believed by the receiver, one must make sure they are following logic.

When studying logic, you will learn how to decipher arguments with mathematical precision. It's the systematic investigation of the principles and application of valid reasoning, and its relations to their conclusions.

Take this for example:
1. If I'm wet, then it's raining
2. It's raining
Conclusion: I'm wet

Do you think this is a valid argument? Maybe at first glance, you might think yes (and frankly, in common speech, if this was said over text then I would deduce you're trying to say that you're wet but that's given the context of casual conversation) however this is NOT valid. This is not valid because the first statement does not mean that every time it's raining, I'm wet, it just means that if I am wet, then it's raining. People who don't have any education in formal logic or argumentation building would be more vulnerable for invalid arguments like this.

Let's do another exercise to not only show you how useful the study of logic is, but also to convince you that my own argument is something valid and sensible. 
G(x): x is a good system of mandatory education
P(x): x includes philosophy
A(x): x adequately prepares students to answer the following questions
- Who am I?
- Am I prepared to be a good and effective citizen, worker, and friend?

The premises would follow as:
1. All good mandatory education systems are systems that adequately prepare students to answer the key life and civic questions. "All G are A". $\forall x (G(x) \rightarrow A(x))$
2. All systems that adequately prepare students to answer the key life and civic questions are systems that include philosophy. "All A are P".  $\forall x (A(x) \rightarrow P(x))$
Conclusion: Therefore, all good mandatory education systems are systems that include philosophy. "Therefore, all G and P." $\forall x (G(x) \rightarrow P(x))$

Almost immediately, you can see why studying logic is imperative for being a well-functioning human being. As we are social beings that rely on one another to make good politics, share goods and services, etc. we are always making claims where we want to be believed to be valid (me to you right now!) Understanding how we can do this clearly and without paradox means we can make better judgements on other people's claims and make claims that are adopted by others. Think about how important it is within friendship-building, democracy, professional settings--we are always in a tug and pull with people around us, in conversations that invoke our rationality to make good decisions. There are too many pitfalls to argumentation that slow this down: Hasty generalizations, straw man attacks, ad hominem, correlation vs. causation, etc. Logic is necessary to sustain foundational structures of common life from these falsities.

Second, let's go to Moral Theory. 

I like to think Moral Theory as the fire in which people live their lives. Without it, there is but a shell of the mechanics of a person--like a tool without a user. Everyone has their own moral theory--maybe flawed or not realized yet--deep down. It's the values we live by which influence what we think are good or bad choices. Maybe you don't believe in moral good or bad. But even that is a value that influences significant decisions that determines your character.

I think it's through Moral Theory people are able to challenge and determine good, long lasting values for their lives. These are values that protect us from feeling non-human, from slaving away at goals that don't actually fulfill us, from being merely a tool other's interact with. These are the same values that also help determine what we want to do professionally or outside our professional life, how we treat other people and what politics to subscribe to. Many of my peers and I personally have often struggled with feeling "lost," "uninspired," and "not sure what I'm doing with my life." These questions and feelings are ones that I'm sure every human with the luxury to think such things will come back to time and time again. These are questions grappled by Moral Theory. I see it as a secular religion open to all.

After Moral Theory, I've also included the following to the list plus brief description of what they are and why they may be included:
- Some of:
	- Political philosophy: What do we owe each other as members of society? A theory fundamental to being a good, effective voting citizen
	- Philosophy of economics: What is rationality/rational choice? Welfare economics/building of a society. Also a theory fundamental to being a good, effective voting citizen and understanding what makes a decision rational.
	- Indigenous or eastern philosophy: To balance the overwhelming representation of western philosophy with one rooted in relationships, responsibility and dignity of nature, spirituality frameworks, etc.
	- Epistemology (philosophy of knowledge): Knowledge vs. belief as well as ethics of belief (intellectual humility). Good inward practice of understanding where our knowledge comes from.
I could imagine logic and moral theory being their own classes, and then these being just mere chapters within some philosophy class.

I am not arguing that everyone who studies philosophy will leave transformed, or that a mandatory class will fix the many failures of modern education. But I do think there is something lost when people move through their entire formal education without ever being asked: _What makes a good argument? What do_ _I_ _actually_ _believe_ _is_ _right,_ _and_ _why?_ _What_ _do_ _I_ _owe_ _the_ _people_ _around_ _me?_ These are not luxury questions. They are the foundation of a life--and a society--worth living in. This is the case I want to leave you with.
